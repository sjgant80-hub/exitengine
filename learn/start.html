I just got laid off. Now what?

The first 72 hours are different from everything that comes after. Get them right and the rest is easier. Get them wrong and you'll spend months undoing it.

The four things you must not do this week

1 · Do not post anything public

No LinkedIn announcement. No "open to work" badge. No mass-DM to your network. The version of you typing right now is the saddest, angriest, most defensive version of you that exists. Anything you post will reflect that. You will read it back in a year and cringe.

Wait at least 7 days. Write a draft if you must. Save it. Re-read it Friday next week. Probably you'll edit half of it. Probably the half you'd edit is the half that would have hurt you.

2 · Do not sign up for anything irreversible

Not a coaching programme. Not a "career accelerator." Not a £2,000 LinkedIn masterclass. Not a CRM annual subscription. Not a new courses platform. Not a paid community.

The grief makes you want to buy a fix. The fix will feel real for 48 hours. Then you'll have spent money that was supposed to last six months on an emotional purchase. Wait 30 days before any paid commitment.

3 · Do not blast your CV out

Mass-applying to 50 roles in week 1 has the same outcome as mass-applying to 5 roles in week 3 — except in week 3 you've thought about what you actually want, and your applications are 10x better.

The job board is not going anywhere. Take the week.

4 · Do not borrow against your future

Not a personal loan. Not a credit-card balance transfer. Not a "just to bridge me" cash advance. Not a payday loan, ever.

If your runway is genuinely tight, the answer is to cut expenses fast, not increase liabilities. The rate you'll pay on debt is far worse than the rate you can earn back through the right consulting gig.

The five things you should do this week

  1. Read your contract. Notice period · pay in lieu · garden leave · non-compete clauses (often unenforceable). Know exactly what you're entitled to before you negotiate the exit.
  2. Check your final payslip. Holiday pay, bonus pro-rata, any deferred comp. The leaving payment is usually wrong the first time.
  3. File for benefits if you qualify. JSA in UK, unemployment insurance in US, equivalents elsewhere. Apply within the first week — you lose backdated weeks if you don't.
  4. Cancel the dispensable. Streaming services you didn't watch. Gym you didn't go to. Subscriptions on auto-renew. Free yourself £80-£200/month of friction.
  5. Sleep. Genuinely. Eight hours minimum. The post-redundancy brain is not at its best, and the work it's about to do (rebuilding) needs a brain that is rested.

The reframe

You're not unemployed. You're between contracts.

That's not motivational fluff. It's accurate. The company was the middleman. They paid you because someone else was paying them. That someone-else still has a problem. You can sell directly to them — at a higher day rate, because there's no longer a middleman taking a margin.

You don't need another employer. You need to find the next client. There's a difference.

When you're ready (could be today, could be three weeks)

Come back. The CRM is waiting. The tutor will walk you through the rebuild — five phases, your pace. There is no schedule. Whatever you do this week is right because you're doing it.
