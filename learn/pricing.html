How to price yourself

The rate that makes sense. The one most people get wrong — they price like an employee who was overpaid, or like a freelancer who is undervaluing themselves. The right rate is between those.

The math nobody shows you

If you were earning £60,000 a year as an employee, that does NOT mean your independent day rate is £60k ÷ 240 working days = £250 per day. Here's why:

What was hidden as an employee% of cost
Employer's National Insurance (UK)~14%
Pension contribution3-10%
Holiday pay (28 days)11%
Sick pay coverage3%
Health insurance / benefits5-10%
Training budget1-3%
Equipment / software / phone2-5%
Total hidden cost~40-55%

That means a £60,000 employee actually costs the employer roughly £84,000-£93,000. To replace that take-home as an independent — pre-tax, before sick days or admin time — you need to charge:

£X / day

Add 20-30% for the fact that you should be earning more as an independent — you're carrying the risk and the variable demand. So the realistic ask is the rate above, but you should not go below it.

Three pricing models · pick one to start

Day rate

Best for ongoing engagements (3+ days per client per month). Easy to quote, easy to understand. UK typical: £400-£1,500/day depending on niche and seniority. US: $600-$2,000/day.

Project / fixed fee

Best for defined deliverables ("audit your pipeline · 5-page report · 2-week turnaround"). Higher margin if you're efficient — you charge for the outcome, not the hours. Hardest part: scoping. Always add 25% buffer to your initial estimate.

Monthly retainer

Best for steady advisory work. You commit X hours/month, client commits Y£/month. Sticky for both sides. UK typical retainer: £1,500-£8,000/month depending on hours and seniority.

The first-client pricing trick

Whatever rate you settle on, your first client gets a discount — but it must be FRAMED as "founding client" or "case-study rate" — never as desperation. Discount no more than 25%. Be explicit it's time-limited (one project, or first 3 months of retainer).

Why: you need the case study and the reference. They get the discount. Everyone wins. Don't make this discount the new baseline.

The rate you should NOT charge

  1. Whatever your old salary worked out to per hour. That was your employee compensation. You're not an employee now.
  2. The rate a junior on Upwork charges. They live in a different cost structure. You're not competing with them.
  3. "Whatever the client can pay." Asymmetric information. They'll always tell you their budget is lower than it is. Quote your rate. Negotiate down by 10-15% only if needed.
  4. £0. Free work attracts free clients. It does not lead to paid work. (Exception: a single, tightly-scoped, named case study with a recognisable brand. Maximum once.)

Now what

Pick a rate. Write it down. Put it in the Config screen. Every invoice, proposal, and outreach you make will use it.

You can always adjust later — that's the point of an independent business. Just start with a number.
