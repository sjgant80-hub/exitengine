Invoicing, tax, getting paid

The four things you must get right. None of them are hard. All of them are unforgiving if you ignore them. Plain English version.

1 · Register correctly

UK

  • Sole trader · default option. Register at gov.uk/register-for-self-assessment. Takes 10 minutes. You file a Self Assessment tax return annually by 31 January for the previous April-April year.
  • Limited company. Worth setting up once you're earning more than ~£40,000/year as a freelancer · saves tax through salary + dividends split. Cost: ~£12 to incorporate at Companies House.
  • VAT. Mandatory once you earn £90,000+ in a 12-month period. Voluntary registration below that — often worth it if your clients are VAT-registered businesses.

US

  • Sole proprietor · default. No registration needed. You file Schedule C with your annual 1040.
  • LLC. $50-$500 to set up depending on state. Provides liability protection. Doesn't change federal tax by default · file Schedule C unless you elect S-corp.
  • S-corp election. Worth it above ~$80,000/year of business profit. Saves Social Security and Medicare tax on the dividend portion.
  • Quarterly estimated tax. You owe IRS estimated tax 4 times a year (April · June · Sept · January). Underpayment penalty if you don't.

Elsewhere

Each country differs. Google "freelancer tax registration {{ your country }}". The principle is the same everywhere: register early, set aside a fixed percentage of every payment for tax, file annually. Get a local accountant for the first year ONLY — £400-£800 once gives you a year of "this is how I do it" knowledge.

2 · Open a separate business bank account

Do not run your business through your personal current account. Six reasons:

  1. Reconciliation. When tax season comes you need to know which transactions were business.
  2. Cash flow visibility. Your business has its own runway, separate from your personal spending.
  3. Professionalism. Invoices that say "Sarah Chen Consulting" land harder than ones that say "Sarah Chen" with a personal sort code.
  4. Tax discipline. Easier to set aside the tax-percentage in a separate account from your spending money.
  5. Audit defence. If HMRC or IRS audits, you can show them a clean business account, not a mixed one.
  6. Mental separation. Personal vs business money should feel different. Same account makes both feel anxious.

UK free options: Tide · Starling Business · Monzo Business (free tier). US free options: Mercury · Novo · Bluevine.

3 · Tax-set-aside discipline

The 30% rule

The minute a client pays you, move 30% of the payment into a separate "tax" account. Never touch it. When tax bill comes, the money is there.

This rule works for most freelance income up to ~£60-80k. Above that, increase to 35%. If you're VAT-registered, also move the VAT element to a separate "VAT" account on receipt.

This single discipline is the difference between freelancers who survive tax season and freelancers who don't.

4 · Invoice well · and chase fast

WhatWhen
Send invoiceSame day work is delivered · not 5 days later
Default payment terms14 days · NOT 30 (you're a freelancer not a multinational)

Firmer chaseDay +7 overdue
Letter Before Action (UK) / Demand Letter (US)Day +14 overdue
County Court / Small ClaimsIf LBA ignored after its 14-day window

You don't need a lawyer to chase money

The Letter Before Action template is published in the UK GroundLevel tool · the US Demand Letter is too. Both are templates with 5 variables. £35-£455 to file at Money Claim Online if needed. Court fees are recoverable from the late-paying client.

80% of late invoices settle at the LBA stage. 15% settle at mediation. 5% reach a hearing. You almost never end up in court.

The simplest workable system

  1. Register as sole trader / sole proprietor today (10 mins, free)
  2. Open free business bank account this week (15 mins, free)
  3. Use the Accounts tool to invoice (free, no monthly fee)
  4. Move 30% of every payment to a "tax" account the day it lands
  5. Use the GroundLevel templates for chasing late payers (free)
  6. Hire an accountant ONCE in your first year (£400-£800) to do the first tax return and teach you the rules

That's it. Total cost: less than a month of one SaaS subscription. Total complexity: under 4 hours of setup. Total result: a legally-clean, financially-disciplined business.
